# CityFHEPS and Supportive Housing Timelines for HRA Tier II Domestic Violence Shelters vs. DHS Single Adults

## Executive Summary

New York City’s CityFHEPS voucher and supportive housing systems treat residents of HRA domestic violence (DV) shelters and DHS single‑adult shelters under largely similar formal eligibility rules, but their lived timelines and access pathways differ because of program design and capacity constraints. Residents of HRA Tier II DV shelters can begin CityFHEPS eligibility screening as soon as they are in shelter and meet income and program criteria, yet data show that only a small minority exit DV emergency shelters directly to permanent housing with a subsidy, and many cycle into other shelters. Single adult men in the DHS system face very long average shelter stays and, if they pursue supportive housing through the NYC Supportive Housing Application (formerly 2010e), can expect a multi‑stage process where clinical eligibility is decided within days but actual placement often takes months or longer.[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5][^6]

## Systems Overview

The CityFHEPS program is a city‑funded rental assistance (voucher‑like) program administered by the Department of Social Services (DSS) through HRA, aimed at helping households in DHS or HRA shelters, as well as some in the community, secure and maintain permanent housing. It operates under Title 68, Chapter 10 of the Rules of the City of New York and now consists of three components: tenant‑based rental assistance, project‑based rental assistance, and a unit‑repair program.[^7][^8][^9][^3]

HRA operates a separate, confidential DV shelter system that includes emergency DV shelters with a state‑mandated 180‑day maximum stay and domestic violence Tier II transitional shelters, which have no statutory time limit and are designed for survivors needing longer‑term stabilization. DHS runs a parallel system of shelters for single adults, adult families, and families with children; single adults are the largest group served, and their average length of stay has exceeded a year.[^10][^2][^11][^12][^1]

Supportive housing in NYC is accessed through a centralized, five‑stage process using the Coordinated Assessment and Placement System (CAPS) and the NYC Supportive Housing Application (formerly called the HRA 2010e), which is reviewed by HRA’s clinical team and then used to make referrals to scattered‑site and congregate supportive housing.[^13][^4][^5]

## HRA DV Emergency and Tier II Shelters

Emergency DV shelters under HRA provide confidential, service‑rich housing to survivors for up to 180 days under New York State regulations; survivors may secure 90 days initially with extensions up to a maximum of 6 months. When survivors reach the 180‑day limit without permanent housing, they may be transferred to an HRA domestic‑violence Tier II transitional shelter or to a DHS shelter, both of which have no statutory time limits on length of stay.[^2][^1][^10]

HRA’s DV system is large and frequently at or near capacity, with dozens of emergency facilities and multiple Tier II sites; recent analyses indicate that in 2023 there were roughly 2,285 emergency beds and 635 Tier II units citywide. Despite this scale, most households leaving DV emergency shelter do not move directly to permanent, subsidized housing, reflecting limited voucher supply and tight rental markets.[^1][^2]

## CityFHEPS Eligibility for Shelter Residents

CityFHEPS is available to households in DHS or HRA shelters that meet income and program criteria and do not qualify for another rental assistance program such as FHEPS. For shelter residents, the core eligibility framework requires that the household have gross income below 200 percent of the federal poverty level, receive Cash Assistance if eligible, and lack access to another rental subsidy.[^14][^15][^3]

Within that framework, the CityFHEPS FAQ for shelter residents (DSS‑7n) explains that a household qualifies through membership in Group A or Group B, with Group A including families or single adults in DHS shelters (under earlier rules, subject to historical 90‑day stay conditions) and any household residing in an HRA shelter or in a DHS shelter but eligible for HRA shelter. Subsequent rule changes eliminated the 90‑day shelter‑stay requirement so that families and single adults in qualifying DHS or HRA shelters, including DV shelters, can be deemed eligible for CityFHEPS without waiting 90 days, provided they meet the other criteria.[^16][^17][^18][^3]

## Milestones for CityFHEPS in HRA Tier II DV Shelters

### 1. Entry into DV Shelter and Transfer to Tier II

Survivors typically first enter an HRA emergency DV shelter via the city’s DV hotline, a community referral, or an assessment at the DHS family intake center by HRA’s No Violence Again (NoVA) staff. After up to 180 days in emergency shelter, survivors who still need housing may be transferred, subject to availability, into HRA domestic‑violence Tier II transitional shelters, which offer longer stays without a statutory cap and continued case management and housing navigation support.[^10][^2][^1]

### 2. Initial CityFHEPS Screening and Income/Program Alignment

Once in an HRA DV shelter (emergency or Tier II), a resident can be screened for CityFHEPS eligibility as a household residing in an HRA shelter, without any minimum stay requirement under current rules. At this stage, the case manager or housing specialist confirms that household income is under 200 percent of the federal poverty level, that the survivor is on Cash Assistance if eligible, and that the household does not qualify for another rental assistance program such as FHEPS.[^18][^3][^7]

If the survivor’s household includes children and they lost housing due to domestic violence, they may simultaneously be assessed for FHEPS, a separate rent supplement for Cash‑Assistance families with children who were evicted or lost housing due to domestic violence or other safety issues. However, CityFHEPS cannot be approved if FHEPS is available, so the caseworker must determine which program the household fits best.[^14]

### 3. Issuance of CityFHEPS “Shopping Letter” and Household Share Letter

When DSS systems identify a shelter household as potentially eligible for CityFHEPS, the agency issues a CityFHEPS “Shopping Letter” and a “Household Share Letter,” which the case manager or housing specialist provides to the client. The Shopping Letter shows the maximum rent DSS will support and outlines landlord incentives and documentation requirements, while the Household Share Letter estimates how much of the rent the household would be responsible for paying based on current income.[^9][^3]

Recent testimony from DSS leadership indicates that for shelter residents, the housing conversation is supposed to begin on day one of shelter stay, with caseworkers updating an independent living plan regularly and housing specialists helping to certify clients for any subsidy they might qualify for; for those who meet initial CityFHEPS criteria, shopping letters are now issued automatically in most cases. An audit by the State Comptroller confirms that shopping letters are generated after data matching between shelter and benefit systems and function as the trigger for the household to begin searching for a unit.[^19][^9]

### 4. Apartment Search and Submission of Full CityFHEPS Packet

Once the survivor receives the Shopping and Household Share letters, it becomes the survivor’s responsibility—supported by the housing specialist—to locate a unit in one of the five boroughs within the CityFHEPS payment standards and that meets basic program rules. The shopping letter is intended to reassure landlords and brokers by specifying maximum rent levels, potential DSS incentives, and the documentation they must supply for the application.[^3][^9]

After the survivor identifies a unit, the housing specialist works with the landlord to assemble and submit the full CityFHEPS packet, including the completed landlord documents, proposed lease, inspection information, and the client’s verifications, so that HRA can make a final eligibility determination and approve the unit for subsidy. HRA may also use a “unit hold” incentive (often one month’s rent) to encourage landlords to hold units while the packet is processed.[^9][^3]

### 5. Final Eligibility Determination, Approval Letter, and Move‑Out

CityFHEPS eligibility is not formally conferred until DSS reviews the complete packet and issues an approval notice showing the final household share and subsidy amount, at which point HRA can pay the landlord and the survivor can move into permanent housing. The program generally allows up to four annual renewals (about five years total), with additional extensions for good cause, and households with an older adult (60+) or a member receiving federal disability benefits are exempt from the typical five‑year maximum.[^3][^9]

In practice, the elapsed time from initial shopping‑letter issuance to CityFHEPS approval and exit to permanent housing can be substantial: a recent audit of a sample of CityFHEPS cases found that, on average, households waited around 10 months between receiving a shopping letter and being approved and exiting shelter, with some cases taking more than three years. DV‑specific data further show that among survivor‑led households leaving emergency DV shelters in 2023, only 9 percent moved to permanent homes and just 74 households exited with some form of rental subsidy (including but not necessarily limited to CityFHEPS), while half moved to another shelter, including Tier II and DHS shelters.[^2][^9]

## Comparison: CityFHEPS for HRA DV vs. DHS Single Adult Men

### Formal Priority and Eligibility

On paper, residents of HRA DV shelters (emergency or Tier II) and single adult men in DHS shelters are treated similarly by CityFHEPS: both are considered shelter residents in qualifying systems and can be assessed for eligibility once they meet the income, Cash Assistance, and program criteria, without any 90‑day minimum stay requirement under current rules. The rule amendments specifically removed the prior 90‑day requirement for both families and single adults to qualify for CityFHEPS and created a uniform 10‑hour‑per‑week work requirement for some categories, rather than distinct standards by shelter type.[^17][^16][^18][^3]

CityFHEPS rules do not create a separate, formal priority tier solely for survivors of domestic violence in HRA shelters, even though domestic violence is a leading cause of homelessness among families in DHS shelters and survivors may have other priority access to federal vouchers and public housing. For example, HPD’s federal Section 8 voucher program and NYCHA’s public housing and voucher programs explicitly give priority or preference to households experiencing homelessness and survivors of domestic violence, but those programs are separate from CityFHEPS and constrained by closed or backlogged waitlists.[^1][^2]

### Practical Timelines for Single Adults in DHS Shelters

Single adults in DHS shelters face long average stays overall: in City Fiscal Year 2023, the average length of stay for single adults in the DHS system was about 412 days, and in the prior fiscal year 2022 it was approximately 509 days. A 2026 review by the City Comptroller similarly notes that single adults are the largest population served by DHS and that their average stays in shelter have consistently exceeded a year, with stays for adult families even longer.[^11][^12][^20]

For CityFHEPS specifically, the Comptroller’s audit of DHS shelter residents found that, across a sample of 52 cases, the time from shopping letter issuance to CityFHEPS approval and exit to permanent housing averaged about 10 months, with one case taking over three years. This suggests that for a single adult male who receives a shopping letter relatively early in his shelter stay, the path from potential eligibility to an actual subsidized move‑out can easily run close to a year or more, depending on unit availability and administrative delays.[^11][^9]

Available evidence does not indicate that single adult men in DHS shelters are systematically deprioritized relative to other shelter residents for CityFHEPS; rather, bottlenecks stem from the limited supply of affordable units within payment standards, administrative inefficiencies in DSS processing, and high overall demand citywide. Nonetheless, because DV survivors in HRA shelters often present as families with children, they may have somewhat better access to family‑sized units and to other housing programs, whereas single men are largely funneled toward smaller CityFHEPS units or supportive housing placements.[^2][^9][^11][^1]

## 2010e/NYC Supportive Housing Pathway for Single Adult Men

### Application and Eligibility Determination

Supportive housing in NYC is accessed through a unified process using CAPS and the NYC Supportive Housing Application (formerly the HRA 2010e), which must be completed and submitted electronically by an approved social service or health provider, such as a DHS shelter case manager, hospital staff, or a mental health professional. The application requires a psychosocial assessment, and for mental‑health–focused supportive housing it also requires a current psychiatric evaluation, along with detailed documentation of clinical conditions, functional impairments, and homelessness history.[^21][^22][^23][^13]

Once the application is submitted, HRA’s clinical social work team reviews it and typically issues an eligibility determination within one to three business days, though missing documentation can prolong this step. Approved applicants receive a determination letter indicating the types of supportive housing programs they qualify for and a standardized vulnerability category (low, medium, or high), which is used in prioritizing referrals to specific projects.[^4][^5]

### Stages and Typical Timelines to Placement

The city’s supportive housing guidance describes a five‑stage process: Coordinated Assessment Survey, Supportive Housing Application, Eligibility Determination, Housing Referral and Placement, and Housing Move‑In, explicitly noting that the process takes time and that there is more demand for supportive housing than available units. Official FAQs emphasize that while eligibility review is relatively quick, the time to actually obtain a supportive housing unit depends on vacancies, the applicant’s needs and preferences, and ongoing documentation updates, and that applicants “may have to wait multiple months or longer” for a suitable unit to become available.[^5][^4]

Advocacy and training materials provide more concrete ranges: a Coalition for the Homeless guide explains that after a 2010e approval, the referring agency begins sending the packet to housing providers and that it can take “several weeks or months” to find an appropriate placement due to limited vacancies. A supportive‑housing training for NYC Health+Hospitals describes the pathway from 2010e submission to placement as involving a 24–48‑hour determination letter followed by a “3–6 month average” from referral to supportive housing placement, again contingent on unit availability and documentation.[^24][^6][^21]

Eligibility approvals themselves must be kept current; some materials indicate that 2010e approvals are valid for six months or, in newer guidance, that eligibility determinations are valid for about twelve months, after which an updated application is required if no placement has occurred. This means that a single adult man whose placement takes longer than these validity windows may need repeated updates, adding procedural friction to an already lengthy process.[^21][^4]

### Relative Priority and Outcomes

The CAPS system uses a standardized vulnerability assessment and centralized vacancy data to prioritize supportive housing referrals, but published guidance does not identify single adult males in general DHS shelters as either a preferred or disfavored group; priority is driven primarily by disability type, chronic homelessness status, and program‑specific eligibility criteria. Some new city initiatives, such as NYC 15/15 congregate supportive housing, target chronically homeless individuals with serious mental illness or substance use disorders, populations that include many single adult men, but strict chronic‑homelessness definitions and documentation requirements can limit access.[^25][^23][^5][^24]

Data from advocacy reporting suggest that, system‑wide, the gap between approved supportive housing applications and actual placements is substantial, with only a minority of approved applicants securing units in a given year and many approvals expiring without placement. For a single adult male, this implies that supportive housing is best viewed as a medium‑term to long‑term pathway with uncertain odds of success, in contrast to CityFHEPS, which—while also slow—has clearer procedural milestones and a more direct link to permanent housing once a unit is located.[^26][^6][^9]

## Comparative Summary: DV Tier II vs. DHS Single Adults and Supportive Housing

### CityFHEPS Milestones and Timelines

For survivors in HRA DV shelters (including Tier II), the key CityFHEPS milestones are: entry into the DV shelter system; CityFHEPS screening and Cash Assistance alignment; issuance of the Shopping and Household Share letters; apartment search and submission of the landlord packet; and final approval and move‑out. Single adult men in DHS shelters follow essentially the same CityFHEPS milestones, with the main structural difference being that they are in the general DHS system rather than the confidential DV system, and they are more likely to be seeking single‑room or studio‑size units.[^9][^3][^11]

In both groups, empirical data show that the bottleneck is not the initial eligibility review but rather the time it takes to find an acceptable unit and to move from shopping letter to approval, which averaged about 10 months across a recent sample of CityFHEPS cases, regardless of shelter type. For DV survivors, that delay interacts with the 180‑day cap on emergency DV shelter stays and limited Tier II capacity, resulting in many survivors transferring into other shelters instead of exiting directly to permanent housing; in 2023, only 9 percent of survivor‑led households leaving emergency DV shelters moved to permanent homes, and just a subset of those did so with rental subsidies.[^2][^9]

### Relative Priority and Practical Access

Formally, CityFHEPS rules no longer embed a 90‑day stay requirement and do not provide a distinct, codified priority tier for HRA DV shelter residents versus single adult men in DHS shelters; both populations are evaluated under the same income and program criteria as shelter residents. However, DV survivors often have access to additional housing pathways—such as Section 8 preferences, NYCHA emergency transfers, and HPD set‑asides for households already in HRA DV shelters—which can indirectly improve their overall housing prospects, even though those systems have their own severe capacity limits and backlogs.[^17][^18][^3][^1][^2]

Single adult men in DHS shelters, by contrast, often rely on CityFHEPS and supportive housing as their primary pathways to permanent housing and face average shelter stays of well over a year, reflecting both the scarcity of units affordable on vouchers and the limited supply of supportive housing slots. The 2010e/NYC Supportive Housing process can deliver a unit within a few months for some, but for many it remains a long‑shot, medium‑term option layered on top of CityFHEPS or other subsidy efforts.[^6][^12][^11]

Overall, both DV survivors in HRA Tier II shelters and single adult men in DHS shelters confront structural shortages of affordable, subsidized housing; formal program rules give them similar access to CityFHEPS, but timelines are dominated by apartment scarcity and administrative delays, while supportive housing for single adults remains a slower, more selective pathway that typically follows a separate clinical eligibility track rather than providing a near‑term alternative to vouchers.[^5][^6][^9]
