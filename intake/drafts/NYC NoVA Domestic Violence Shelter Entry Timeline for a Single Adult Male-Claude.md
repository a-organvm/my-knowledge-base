# NYC NoVA Domestic Violence Shelter Entry Timeline for a Single Adult Male

A single adult man who is screened by New York City’s No Violence Again (NoVA) program is **not** placed directly into a private, lockable Tier II domestic-violence apartment on the day of his intake interview. Instead, NoVA typically first places eligible survivors into the emergency domestic-violence shelter system or, when that is not possible, into the general Department of Homeless Services (DHS) single‑adult shelter system with safety accommodations, and only a subset of residents later transfer into Tier II apartments subject to strict eligibility and capacity limits.[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5]

## NoVA and the NYC DV shelter structure

NoVA (“No Violence Again”) is an HRA program embedded in DHS intake centers that assesses people presenting with domestic violence concerns, provides crisis counseling, and determines whether they qualify for placement in HRA-administered domestic violence shelters. Testimony to the City Council describes three intake pathways into the DV shelter system: via the Safe Horizon hotline, via NoVA screening at DHS intake centers (including the single‑adult intake center), and via community‑based referrals from places like hospitals and Family Justice Centers. The city’s DV shelter system is two‑tiered: 42 confidential emergency DV shelters plus 12 family transitional (Tier II) shelters, providing about 2,290 emergency DV beds and 628 Tier II units across the five boroughs.[^2][^3][^6][^7][^1]

The City Council has made clear that only survivors who are first stabilized in the emergency DV system are eligible to transfer into Tier II shelters; direct entry into Tier II from intake is not how the system is designed. A recent Local Law 83 report shows that in 2024 the DV system served 11,853 individuals in 4,526 households, of whom only 484 were single adults, indicating that most DV capacity is geared toward families with children rather than single adults. In response to gaps, HRA is planning to add 94 new emergency DV beds with a specific emphasis on single‑adult households and LGBTQ survivors, but these facilities still cannot be limited exclusively to singles due to state rules and must also accept families.[^8][^5][^2]

## Intake day and immediate placement (Day 1)

### Step 1: Presenting for shelter and NoVA screening

Single adults who need any NYC shelter are directed to DHS intake centers, including the 30th Street Intake Center for men. At these intake sites, DHS staff conduct a general homelessness intake and then, if the person reports domestic violence or staff suspect it, they refer the person to NoVA for a specialized DV assessment. The NoVA assessment can involve a detailed interview (often conducted by phone for safety) to explore the abuse history, current risk, and whether DV‑specific shelter is appropriate.[^9][^10][^11][^1][^2]

In addition to the intake‑center route, survivors can also reach the DV shelter system by calling the city’s 24‑hour Safe Horizon DV hotline, which connects callers to DV shelter referrals, or through other community providers who then link to HRA/DHS. However, for a single adult male already at a DHS intake site, the practical sequence is: general intake, then NoVA screening if DV is disclosed, then placement decisions based on DV eligibility and bed availability.[^3][^6][^12][^10][^11][^1]

### Step 2: Same‑day emergency placement decision

DHS’s Guide to Services notes that after intake, single adults are placed into an assessment or other temporary shelter while eligibility and the most appropriate placement are determined. In parallel, NoVA staff decide whether the person qualifies for an HRA domestic‑violence emergency shelter or should instead remain in the DHS single‑adult system with enhanced safety planning (for example, placement far from the abuser). When domestic‑violence beds are available and the survivor meets criteria, NoVA can arrange same‑day or near‑same‑day placement into a confidential emergency DV shelter; when DV beds are unavailable or criteria are not met, the person is generally placed in a DHS single‑adult shelter with DV‑informed services rather than in a DV‑specific facility.[^4][^10][^11][^1]

## Emergency DV shelter conditions (functional “Tier I”)

New York City law distinguishes between “domestic violence emergency shelters” (time‑limited emergency housing) and “domestic violence Tier II shelters” (longer‑term transitional housing subject to Part 900 family shelter regulations). City officials describe the emergency DV network as 42 confidential facilities providing trauma‑informed services for survivors at immediate risk, with 2,290 beds for individuals and families. State guidance for DV residential programs allows emergency stays of up to 90 days, with possible extensions (for example, an additional 45 days per certain conditions), underscoring the short‑term, crisis‑stabilization role of these placements.[^13][^14][^2]

Because DV shelter addresses are confidential, public documents focus on general design patterns rather than floor plans, but several NYC providers and hearings describe typical layouts. Many DV emergency shelters are “shared living” environments where each household has its own private sleeping quarters but shares bathrooms, kitchens, and living spaces with other residents. For example, Henry Street Settlement’s DV shelter in Manhattan consists of 13 furnished apartments, each shared by two families, with residents receiving on‑site services and a time‑limited stay while they work toward permanent housing. Other New York State DV shelters similarly report that families generally have their own bedroom and often their own bathroom, while sharing kitchen, dining, and common living areas with other residents.[^15][^16][^17][^18][^19][^20][^9]

Security in DV emergency shelters is typically intensive. Providers describe 24/7 on‑site staff, controlled entry, and strict confidentiality protocols, including prohibitions on disclosing the shelter’s physical address. For instance, AAPCI notes that addresses, as well as residents’ names, are treated as strictly confidential and that trained security specialists provide round‑the‑clock protection at their DV facilities. Similar language appears across DV shelter providers nationwide, emphasizing secure entrances, visitor restrictions, and confidentiality, even when the shelter itself is not in a fully undisclosed location.[^21][^22][^18][^9]

For a single adult male placed into a DV emergency shelter, this usually means a private or semi‑private bedroom within a locked, staff‑supervised building, with shared bathrooms and shared kitchen or common spaces, rather than a self‑contained private apartment. The building is generally secured by controlled entry and staff presence, but the room itself is not a long‑term leasehold and is part of a communal environment designed for short‑term safety and stabilization.[^16][^14][^18][^9][^13]

## DHS single‑adult shelters when DV beds are unavailable

If NoVA cannot place the survivor into an HRA DV shelter, the default for a single adult man is the DHS single‑adult shelter system, which has a different physical setup and less built‑in privacy. DHS states that “most single adult shelters are congregate settings” where residents share facilities, with dormitory‑style rooms that typically house 5 to 20 clients. Bathrooms, showers, and common areas are shared, and meals are usually served cafeteria‑style rather than cooked in individual kitchens.[^1][^9]

All DHS shelters, including single‑adult sites, are required to have 24/7 security and on‑site social services to help residents move toward more permanent housing. However, unlike DV‑specific shelters, addresses of DHS shelters are public, and dorm‑style sleeping arrangements mean a significantly lower level of privacy compared with the apartment‑like configurations common in DV emergency and Tier II shelters. Advocates and survivors have noted that NYC’s current DV system has very limited options for single adults without children, which often forces them into the general DHS system even when they have significant DV‑related safety concerns.[^23][^11][^9][^8][^1]

## Tier II apartments and transfer rules

City law defines “domestic violence Tier II shelters” as DV housing programs subject to the same Part 900 regulations that govern family Tier II shelters, with a focus on longer‑term transitional housing and independent living skills. City Council testimony indicates there are 12 such Tier II DV shelters in NYC, offering 628 family transitional units alongside the emergency DV network. These Tier II programs are explicitly described as an “extension of shelter services” for families who have already been in emergency DV shelter and still need support obtaining safe permanent housing.[^24][^21][^2][^13]

Provider descriptions show that Tier II DV shelters tend to be fully independent apartments rather than dormitories. For example, Serenity House Family Residence, a transitional Tier II shelter, provides 40 “independent living apartments” configured as studios, one‑bedroom, and two‑bedroom units, each with its own full bathroom, full kitchen with refrigerator and gas range, and an open living/dining room. Similarly, the Renee Steedley Family Residence, another Tier II DV program, offers 54 independent apartments for survivors and their children. Other Tier II projects such as Urban Resource Institute’s “Brighter Days” shelter in Queens likewise describe multi‑story buildings composed of independent apartments for families, with on‑site services and high‑security entry.[^25][^21][^9]

Crucially, entry into Tier II DV shelters is **not** direct from NoVA intake. City testimony states that “only domestic violence survivors who are stabilized in the emergency system can be transferred to tier 2 shelters,” confirming that Tier II is a second‑stage placement following emergency DV shelter. Provider materials reinforce this: AAPCI notes that Serenity House and Renee Steedley house families “who have exceeded their maximum length of stay (90 days) in an emergency shelter,” indicating that residents come from prior emergency placements rather than intake. Given that DV Tier II units are designed for families with minor children and that there are only 628 such units citywide, a single adult male without children would be very unlikely to be prioritized for a Tier II DV apartment, even after time in the emergency system.[^5][^21][^2][^8]

## Practical timeline in physical terms

In practice, the physical timeline for a single adult man entering the NoVA DV system generally looks like this:

1. **Intake day (Day 1):** He presents at a DHS intake center (for men, usually the 30th Street Intake Center), discloses DV, and is referred to NoVA for assessment. He spends that first night either in a DHS assessment shelter or, if a DV placement is arranged quickly, transported to a confidential emergency DV shelter.[^10][^11][^4][^1]
2. **Emergency‑shelter phase (days to months):** If placed in an HRA DV emergency shelter, he lives in a secured, staff‑supervised building where he has a private or semi‑private bedroom but shares kitchen, bathroom, and living spaces with other residents. This stay is time‑limited, often up to around 90 days with possible extensions, while caseworkers focus on safety planning and future housing options.[^14][^17][^9][^24][^16]
3. **Possible transition or exit:** Some survivors (primarily families with children) are later transferred into Tier II DV shelters, where they move into independent, lockable apartments within a secure building. Others exit from emergency DV shelter or DHS shelters into permanent housing, other transitional housing, or the general DHS system without ever receiving a Tier II placement.[^26][^21][^9][^5][^25]

For a single adult male without children, most available evidence suggests that the **default expectation** should be an emergency DV or DHS single‑adult congregate placement, not a direct move into a private Tier II apartment, and that any move to a Tier II DV apartment would be exceptional and contingent on both eligibility and severe capacity constraints.[^2][^8][^5]
